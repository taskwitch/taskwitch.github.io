In this very first post (on the website built on graduation from high school), I will share an interesting problem that underlies an AP Computer Science A question my friend asked me during my senior year in high school.

True or false: will i sometimes equal 3?

i = 3
j = 2
for _ in range(100):
  i -= j	
  j += i

The answer is true. The above operation is actually periodic (with a periodicity of $6$), regardless of the values i and j are initially set to!

Matrix Representation of a Linear Transformation#

Here, i and j are manipulated linearly; one probably wants to write the operation as a linear transformation, thus a matrix transformation.

First, write the iteration equations

$$ i_{k+1} = i_{k} - j_k,\\ j_{k+1} = j_k + i_{k + 1} = j_k + (i_k - j_k) = i_k $$

or equivalently, in a vector form

$$ \begin{pmatrix}i\\j\end{pmatrix}_{k+1} = \begin{pmatrix}i_k - j_k\\i_k\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1 & -1\\1 & 0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}i\\j\end{pmatrix}_{k} $$

Here we denote

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & -1\\1 & 0\end{pmatrix}, \quad \boldsymbol x_k = \begin{pmatrix}i\\j\end{pmatrix}_k $$

Hence

$$ \boldsymbol x_k = A^k \boldsymbol x_0 $$

Periodicity of Linear Transformation#

In order to show periodicity in the linear transformation $A$, there must be $\boldsymbol x^p = A^p\boldsymbol{x_0} = I\boldsymbol x_0 \Rightarrow A^p = I$ for some positive integer $p$.

Even though one may first discover that $A^3 = -I$ (which is suffice to show $A^6 = I$ therefore $A$ has a periodicity of $6$), we are giving a more concrete proof here using eigenvalues.

An eigenvalue for a linear transformation $T$ is some $\lambda$ such that applying $T$ on some non-zero vector $\boldsymbol v$ has the same result as multiplying $\lambda$ on it.

$$ \lambda \in \mathbb F,\quad\text{s.t.}\quad T\boldsymbol v = \lambda \boldsymbol v $$

If an eigenvalue $\lambda$ of some linear transformation $T$ satisfies $\lambda^p=1$, applying $T$ on $\boldsymbol v$ $p$ times gets the original $\boldsymbol v$ back (which implies $T^p = I$). Therefore, linear transformation $T$ has a periodicity of $p$, and this is independent from the selection of non-zero vector $\boldsymbol v$.

One can find the eigenvalues for a matrix by solving its characteristic equation

$$ \det (A - \lambda I) = 0 $$

Plug into our matrix $A$ yields

$$ \begin{aligned} \det \begin{pmatrix}1 - \lambda & -1 \\ 1 & 0 - \lambda \end{pmatrix} &= 0\\ (1 - \lambda)(0 - \lambda) - (-1)(1) &= 0\\ \lambda^2 - \lambda + 1 &= 0\\ \Rightarrow \lambda_{1,2} = \frac{1 \pm \sqrt 3\mathrm i}{2} \end{aligned} $$

(where $\mathrm i$ is the imaginary unit satisfying $\mathrm i^2 = -1$)

Recall that the $n$-th root of unity has the form $\exp(2\pi \mathrm i /n)$, namely

$$ x^n = 1 \Rightarrow x = \exp\left(\frac{2\pi\mathrm i}{n}\right) $$

Take the positive root $\lambda = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{\sqrt 3}{2}\mathrm i$. We compare it to the right-hand-side of the Euler’s formula

$$ \exp (\mathrm i \theta) = \cos \theta + \mathrm i \sin \theta $$

Then one gets $\theta = \pi / 3$. Plug into the left-hand-side yields

$$ \lambda = \exp\left(\mathrm i \frac{\pi}{3}\right) = \exp\left(\frac{2\pi \mathrm i}{6}\right) $$

which is exactly the $6$-th root of unity.

Geometrically, if we put the complex number $\lambda$ on the complex plane, then powering it up is spinning

  • on the unit circle $r = 1$ ($r = |\lambda| = \sqrt{(\frac{1}{2})^2 + (\frac{\sqrt 3}{2})^2} = 1$)
  • at a fixed angle which is a rational fraction of $2\pi$ ($\theta = \arctan (\Im \lambda / \Re \lambda) = 2\pi / 6$)

which is the geometrical behavior of $n$-th root of unity.

Transition matrices $A$ with eigenvalues violating any of the above properties will not show periodicity, as the vector on the complex plane will scale in magnitude or spine at an angle that prevents it from returning the the initial angle.
